Title:

It's (on) the bee's knees! The effect of local habitat and time on pollen foraging efficiency in Bombus impatiens

Abstract:

Bees are one of the most important pollinators in the world, but their diversity and numbers have been in decline for reasons including land-use changes such as large-scale agriculture, pesticide use, and urbanization. This decline has led to investigations of the necessary factors in a bee's habitat and landscape to keep a hive alive; because while bees are hypothetically able to travel up to 2km to forage, it may not actually be ideal for them. This study assessed the variation in Bombus impatiens colony performance and pollen foraging success across a single landscape, among colonies with contrasting vegetation within 500m of a colony but similar vegetation on the large-scale. To do this, pollen was regularly collected, bees were marked with oil paint, and video was taken of every hive in the morning and afternoon. Videos were processed by hand and then run through several statistical tests. I found that the three habitat types had significant differences in hive-level foraging success and survival, but that these changes were not necessarily reflected in the durations of individual foraging trips.
